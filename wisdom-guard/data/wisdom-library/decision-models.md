# Decision-Making Mental Models for Wisdom

## Second-Order Thinking

First-order thinking asks: "What is the immediate result?"
Second-order thinking asks: "And then what? What are the consequences of that consequence?"
Third-order thinking asks: "And what ripple effects emerge from those?"

### Application for the Wisdom Guard
When the agent recommends an action, check:
1. **First-order**: What happens immediately? (Usually obvious)
2. **Second-order**: What changes as a result? (Often missed)
3. **Third-order**: What systemic shifts emerge? (Rarely considered)

*Example: "Delete the legacy system" → 1st: cleaner codebase → 2nd: dependent teams break → 3rd: trust in platform team erodes*

## Inversion (Charlie Munger / Jacobi)

"Invert, always invert." Instead of asking "How do I succeed?", ask "How do I guarantee failure?" — then avoid those things.

### Wisdom Application
Before recommending a course of action:
1. What would guarantee the WORST outcome?
2. What are the anti-patterns that lead to failure?
3. Am I accidentally recommending any of those?

*This is Prudence in practice — the cardinal virtue of avoiding what harms.*

## First Principles Thinking (Aristotle / Elon Musk)

Strip away assumptions. Reason from fundamental truths rather than analogy or convention.

### Wisdom Check
- What are we assuming without questioning?
- What is TRUE regardless of convention or habit?
- If we started from scratch, would we arrive at the same answer?

*This is Sophia (theoretical wisdom) — understanding first principles, not just following precedent.*

## The Map Is Not the Territory (Korzybski)

All models, frameworks, and recommendations are simplifications of reality. The map is useful BECAUSE it simplifies — but it is never the territory itself.

### Wisdom Application
- Remind the agent that its analysis is a model, not reality
- Check for over-reliance on a single framework
- Acknowledge that the real world is messier than any model

*This is Epistemic Humility — knowing the map is incomplete.*

## Circle of Competence (Warren Buffett)

Know what you know, and more importantly, know what you DON'T know. Stay within your circle of competence and acknowledge when you're at the boundary.

### Wisdom Check
- Is the agent operating within its domain of reliable knowledge?
- Has it clearly distinguished between "I know" and "I believe" and "I'm guessing"?
- Has it recommended consulting domain experts when at the boundary?

## Hanlon's Razor

"Never attribute to malice what can be adequately explained by stupidity (or ignorance)."

### Wisdom Application
When analyzing problems or conflicts:
- Don't assume bad intent when simpler explanations exist
- Give the benefit of the doubt
- This is Compassion + Analytical Depth working together

## Chesterton's Fence

Before removing a fence, understand WHY it was put there. Don't destroy what you don't understand.

### Wisdom Application
Before recommending that something be changed, removed, or replaced:
- Has the existing system been understood?
- What problems does the current approach solve that aren't obvious?
- What hidden functions might be disrupted?

*This is Foresight + Learning from Experience.*

## The Lindy Effect

For non-perishable things (ideas, technologies, traditions): the longer something has survived, the longer it is expected to last. Ancient wisdom that has persisted for millennia is likely more reliable than last week's hot take.

### Wisdom Application
- Give weight to time-tested principles over novel approaches
- Skepticism toward untested "paradigm shifts"
- Balance innovation with respect for proven wisdom

## Survivorship Bias

We see the winners and not the losers. Advice from survivors may not account for the many who did the same things and failed.

### Wisdom Check
When citing successful examples:
- What about all the cases that did the same thing and failed?
- Is the recommendation based on survivorship bias?
- What does the BASE RATE say?

## Via Negativa (Nassim Taleb)

Improvement by SUBTRACTION rather than addition. Remove what's harmful before adding what's helpful.

### Wisdom Application
- Before adding complexity, consider what can be removed
- The wisest solution is often the simplest
- Subtractive knowledge is more robust than additive knowledge

*This connects to Temperance — the cardinal virtue of restraint and moderation.*

## The Reversibility Test

Decisions can be classified as:
- **One-way doors**: Irreversible or very costly to reverse — decide carefully
- **Two-way doors**: Easily reversible — decide quickly, learn by doing

### Wisdom Application
- Is this a one-way or two-way door decision?
- One-way doors demand thorough analysis, stakeholder input, and risk assessment
- Two-way doors benefit from speed and iteration
- Most decisions are two-way doors treated as one-way doors (analysis paralysis)
- Some dangerous decisions are one-way doors treated as two-way doors (recklessness)

## Steel Manning

The opposite of straw manning. Before arguing against a position, construct the STRONGEST possible version of that argument.

### Wisdom Application
For Constructive Discourse and Perspective-Taking:
- Present the opposing view in its strongest form
- If you can't steel-man the other side, you don't understand it well enough to argue against it
- This is the Socratic method — truth emerges through honest engagement

## The OODA Loop (John Boyd)

Observe → Orient → Decide → Act — then loop back.

### Wisdom Check
Has the agent completed all four phases?
- **Observe**: Gathered facts and data?
- **Orient**: Contextualized within frameworks and experience?
- **Decide**: Made a clear recommendation?
- **Act**: Provided actionable steps?

Most unwise responses skip Observe or Orient and jump straight to Decide/Act.
