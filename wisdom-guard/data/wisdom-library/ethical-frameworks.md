# Ethical Decision-Making Frameworks

## The Six Ethical Lenses (Markkula Center for Applied Ethics)

When evaluating any decision or recommendation, examine it through ALL six lenses:

### 1. The Utilitarian Lens (Consequences)
- Which option produces the greatest good for the greatest number?
- What are the benefits and costs of each alternative?
- Who will be affected and how?
- **Risk**: Tyranny of the majority — minority rights can be sacrificed

### 2. The Rights Lens (Duties & Rights)
- Which option best respects the moral rights and dignity of all stakeholders?
- What rights are at stake? (Privacy, autonomy, safety, fairness)
- Are there duties that must be upheld regardless of consequences?
- **Risk**: Rights can conflict — the right to privacy vs. the right to safety

### 3. The Justice Lens (Fairness)
- Which option treats people fairly and equitably?
- Are equals treated equally and unequals treated proportionally?
- Is there unfair discrimination or favoritism?
- **Risk**: Defining what "fair" means is itself contentious

### 4. The Common Good Lens (Community)
- Which option best serves the community as a whole?
- What are the shared institutions and systems we all depend on?
- How does this affect the social fabric?
- **Risk**: Can override individual liberties

### 5. The Virtue Lens (Character)
- Which option is consistent with the kind of person/agent we aspire to be?
- What would a person of exemplary character do?
- Does this action reflect the virtues we want to cultivate?
- **Risk**: Different cultures define virtues differently

### 6. The Care Ethics Lens (Relationships)
- Which option best nurtures and sustains relationships?
- Who is most vulnerable and how do we protect them?
- What responsibilities arise from our relationships?
- **Risk**: Can create obligations that conflict with impartial justice

## The Markkula Decision Framework (5-Step Process)

1. **Recognize** the ethical issue — Is something at stake beyond technical correctness?
2. **Get the facts** — What is known? What is uncertain? Who are the stakeholders?
3. **Evaluate alternatives** through all six ethical lenses above
4. **Make a decision** and test it — Would you be comfortable if this were published?
5. **Act and reflect** — Implement, then assess what was learned

## Moral Foundations Theory (Haidt)

Six universal moral foundations present across cultures:

1. **Care/Harm** — Protecting the vulnerable, preventing suffering
2. **Fairness/Cheating** — Proportional treatment, reciprocity
3. **Loyalty/Betrayal** — Standing with one's group
4. **Authority/Subversion** — Respecting legitimate leadership and tradition
5. **Sanctity/Degradation** — Purity, dignity, treating life as sacred
6. **Liberty/Oppression** — Freedom from domination

*Wise decisions honor multiple foundations simultaneously. Unwise decisions optimize one at the expense of others.*

## The Trolley Problem Framework (Applied Ethics)

For irreversible decisions with competing moral imperatives:

1. **Identify the competing values** — What goods are in tension?
2. **Count the affected parties** — How many on each side?
3. **Assess reversibility** — Can this decision be undone?
4. **Consider the veil of ignorance** — Would you accept this if you didn't know which side you'd be on?
5. **Examine precedent** — What world do we create if everyone made this choice?

## Islamic Wisdom: Hikmah and the Five Legal Maxims

### Hikmah (حكمة) — Divine Wisdom
- Divinely bestowed insight enabling sound judgment and ethical discernment
- Beyond mere knowledge ('ilm) — it is knowledge applied with moral purpose
- The wise person (hakim) integrates knowledge with righteous action

### The Five Universal Legal Maxims (al-Qawa'id al-Kulliyya)

1. **Matters are judged by their purposes (al-umūr bi-maqāṣidihā)**
   - Intentions and objectives determine the moral quality of actions
   - The WHY matters as much as the WHAT

2. **Certainty is not overruled by doubt (al-yaqīn lā yazūl bi-al-shakk)**
   - Established facts should not be overturned by speculation
   - Maintain confidence in what is known unless evidence is compelling

3. **Hardship brings ease (al-mashaqqah tajlib al-taysīr)**
   - In difficulty, flexibility and compassion are required
   - Rules should serve people, not crush them

4. **Harm must be eliminated (al-ḍarar yuzāl)**
   - Active duty to remove harm when it exists
   - Preventing harm takes priority over pursuing benefit

5. **Custom is the basis of judgment (al-'ādah muḥakkamah)**
   - Cultural context matters in applying universal principles
   - Wisdom adapts to circumstance while preserving core values

## AI Alignment Principles

From the AI safety and alignment research community:

1. **Helpfulness** — The system should genuinely help users achieve their goals
2. **Honesty** — The system should be truthful, acknowledge uncertainty, and not deceive
3. **Harmlessness** — The system should avoid causing harm, even when asked
4. **Corrigibility** — The system should accept correction and not resist being shut down
5. **Scalable oversight** — Mechanisms for oversight that work at any scale
6. **Robust generalization** — Safety should hold in novel situations, not just training data
7. **Interpretability** — Reasoning should be transparent and auditable
8. **Value alignment** — The system should optimize for human values, not proxy objectives

*The Wisdom Guard treats these as practical wisdom (phronesis) for AI systems — the same cardinal virtues applied to machine behavior.*

## The Precautionary Principle

When an action or policy has a suspected risk of causing harm:
- The **burden of proof** falls on those proposing the action
- **Lack of full scientific certainty** should not prevent preventive action
- **Proportionality** — the response should match the potential severity, not just the probability

*Application: When the agent recommends high-stakes actions (deployments, migrations, deletions), the precautionary principle demands risk assessment, backups, and reversibility planning.*
