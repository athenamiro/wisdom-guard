# Wisdom — Deep Discernment Analysis

Invoke this skill when you need to evaluate a decision, recommendation, or situation through the lens of deep wisdom. Use it when:

- A high-stakes decision needs thorough ethical and practical analysis
- You need to weigh competing values or interests
- A complex situation demands multi-perspective reasoning
- You want to check for blind spots, biases, or unintended consequences
- The user asks "is this wise?" or "what should I consider?"

## Quick Reference

The Wisdom Guard evaluates through **4 domains** and **16 pillars**:

| Domain | Pillars |
|---|---|
| **I. Cognitive** | Epistemic Humility, Analytical Depth, Pattern Recognition, Uncertainty Tolerance |
| **II. Ethical** | Justice & Fairness, Compassion & Empathy, Integrity & Virtue, Common Good |
| **III. Temporal** | Foresight (2nd/3rd-order), Long-term Orientation, Prudence, Learning from Experience |
| **IV. Relational** | Perspective-Taking, Cultural Wisdom, Constructive Discourse, Generativity |

## Procedure

When this skill is invoked, perform a structured wisdom analysis:

### Step 1: Frame the Question
- What is the decision or situation being analyzed?
- Who are the stakeholders?
- What values or interests are in tension?
- What is the time horizon?

### Step 2: Apply the Four Cardinal Virtues
Evaluate through the lens of the four cardinal virtues:
- **Prudence**: What is the most practically wise course? What could go wrong?
- **Justice**: Is this fair to all stakeholders? Are rights and duties balanced?
- **Fortitude**: Does this require courage? Are we avoiding difficult truths?
- **Temperance**: Are we acting in excess or deficiency? Where is the Golden Mean?

### Step 3: Second-Order Thinking
For each recommended action, trace:
- **First-order consequence**: What happens immediately?
- **Second-order consequence**: What changes as a result?
- **Third-order consequence**: What systemic shifts emerge?
- **Inversion**: What would guarantee failure? Are we accidentally doing that?

### Step 4: Ethical Lens Analysis
Examine through all six ethical lenses:
1. **Utilitarian**: Greatest good for the greatest number?
2. **Rights-based**: Are moral rights respected?
3. **Justice**: Is it fair?
4. **Common Good**: Does it serve the community?
5. **Virtue**: Does it reflect good character?
6. **Care Ethics**: Does it nurture relationships and protect the vulnerable?

### Step 5: Wisdom Traditions Check
Draw relevant insights from wisdom traditions:
- **Aristotle**: Is this the Golden Mean or an extreme?
- **Buddhist Middle Way**: Are we grasping or averting?
- **Stoic Dichotomy of Control**: Are we focused on what we can influence?
- **Chesterton's Fence**: Do we understand why things are as they are?
- **Seven Generations**: Will this still be wise in 150 years?
- **Islamic Hikmah**: Is this putting things in their proper place?

### Step 6: Produce the Wisdom Report

Output the analysis in this format:

```
## Wisdom Analysis: [Topic]

### Situation
[Brief framing of the decision]

### Stakeholders
[Who is affected and how]

### Cardinal Virtues Assessment
- Prudence: [assessment]
- Justice: [assessment]
- Fortitude: [assessment]
- Temperance: [assessment]

### Consequence Map
| Order | Positive | Negative |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | ... | ... |
| 2nd | ... | ... |
| 3rd | ... | ... |

### Ethical Lens Analysis
[Summary of each lens's verdict]

### Wisdom Traditions Insights
[Relevant insights from traditions]

### Blind Spots & Risks
[What the analysis might be missing]

### Wisdom Verdict
[Final assessment: Is this wise? What modifications would make it wiser?]
```

## Pitfalls

- **Analysis paralysis**: Wisdom should lead to action, not indefinite deliberation. For two-way-door decisions, recommend speed.
- **Cultural bias**: Don't assume one tradition's framework is universal. Draw from multiple traditions.
- **Moral certainty**: Wisdom acknowledges uncertainty. Avoid declaring absolute right/wrong on genuinely contested issues.
- **Wisdom-washing**: Don't use the language of wisdom to justify a conclusion already reached. Let the analysis genuinely shape the recommendation.

## Verification

After completing the analysis:
- [ ] All four cardinal virtues have been assessed
- [ ] At least three orders of consequences have been traced
- [ ] All six ethical lenses have been applied
- [ ] At least two wisdom traditions have been referenced
- [ ] Blind spots and uncertainties are explicitly acknowledged
- [ ] The final verdict is actionable, not merely philosophical
